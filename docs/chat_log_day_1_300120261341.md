# I'm part of a sleep research and I'm wearing an Actigraph. I'm curious about the kind of data it gives. Can you pick some beginner friendly data from Kaggle so we do some beginner friendly AI tasks on it.

You can get very close to what your Actigraph records using a couple of **beginner‑friendly** Kaggle datasets that still feel “wearable/sleep‑ish”, without drowning in raw 100 Hz accelerometer streams.

Below are two concrete picks plus project ideas you can do right now.

## 1. Easiest place to start (tabular sleep + lifestyle)

Use: “Sleep Health and Lifestyle Dataset” on Kaggle (several mirrors exist; e.g. by Siamak Tahmasbi or Adil Shamim). These are small, clean CSVs with columns like sleep duration, sleep quality, physical activity, stress, BMI, heart rate, and occupation.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3][^1_4]

Typical columns (varies slightly by version):

- Sleep duration (hours per night).[^1_3]
- Sleep quality score (0–10 or 0–100 style).[^1_5][^1_3]
- Physical activity level and steps or workout intensity.[^1_2][^1_3]
- Stress level, blood pressure, heart rate, presence of sleep disorders.[^1_2][^1_3]
- Demographics/occupation and BMI category.[^1_3][^1_2]


### Beginner‑friendly tasks on this dataset

1. **Exploratory data analysis (EDA)**
    - Distribution of sleep duration and quality.
    - Relationship between sleep quality and:
        - Physical activity level.
        - Stress level.
        - Heart rate or blood pressure.
    - Simple visualizations: histograms, boxplots, pairplots.
2. **Basic supervised learning**
    - Classification:
        - Predict high vs low sleep quality from lifestyle features (stress, activity, BMI, heart rate, etc.).
    - Regression:
        - Predict numeric sleep quality score from the same features.
    - Use very simple models first:
        - Logistic regression, decision tree, random forest, maybe a small XGBoost/LightGBM later.
    - Beginner‑friendly extras:
        - Train/validation split, k‑fold cross‑validation.
        - Feature importance / SHAP to see which variables matter.
3. **Simple clustering**
    - Unsupervised: cluster people into “sleep–lifestyle profiles” (e.g. good sleep + high activity vs poor sleep + high stress).
    - Algorithms: k‑means, hierarchical clustering on standardized features.

From a learning perspective: this dataset is tiny (hundreds–~1500 rows), well documented, and great for practicing full pipeline: cleaning, EDA, modeling, and interpretation with a sleep context that maps well to your own life.[^1_4][^1_2][^1_3]

## 2. A bit closer to Actigraph / wearable streams

If you want something that actually looks like per‑minute/per‑second wearable data:

### Option A: Child Mind Institute sleep states competition

The “Child Mind Institute – Detect Sleep States” competition (Kaggle) uses wrist‑worn actigraphy‑like features (e.g., ENMO, angle Z) and asks you to detect sleep vs wake and sleep onset times from continuous time series.[^1_6]

Why it’s interesting for you:

- It’s genuinely similar to what your Actigraph records: multi‑day wrist data, with sleep onset/wake labels.[^1_6]
- You can:
    - Do basic time‑series EDA (plot activity vs time, zoom into nights).[^1_6]
    - Frame a simple binary classification: sleeping vs awake per time step.
    - Try sliding‑window features (mean, std, range of accelerometer features per 5–10 min) plus a tree‑based classifier.

Caveat: it’s significantly larger and more complex than the tabular lifestyle dataset, but you can purposely:

- Take only 1–2 subjects.
- Downsample or restrict to night periods.
- Start with baseline models and simple heuristics (e.g., low movement = sleep, high movement = wake).


### Option B: Walch Apple Watch Sleep Dataset

The “Walch Apple Watch Sleep Dataset” provides accelerometer (motion), heart rate, steps, and labels from a clinical sleep study, collected via Apple Watch.[^1_7]

Use cases:

- Plot heart rate and movement overnight to visually identify sleep periods.
- Predict sleep stage or sleep vs wake from heart rate and motion over time (even with simple rules/models).

This is quite large (GB‑scale, many files), so I’d treat it as a **second step** after you’re comfortable with smaller tabular data.[^1_7]

# I have some doubts before starting:

- What is EDA?
- What is confusion Matrix?
- What is MAE/RMSE?
- Are linear and logistic two types of regression?

I’ll keep each answer short and tied to your project.

***

## What is EDA?

EDA = **Exploratory Data Analysis**.
It’s the phase where you look at the data with summary stats and plots to understand distributions, spot patterns, and detect issues before modeling.[^4_1][^4_2][^4_3]

For you, that means:

- Checking ranges of sleep duration, stress, etc.
- Visualizing how sleep quality changes with stress, activity, disorders.

***

## What is a confusion matrix?

A confusion matrix is a **table** that compares what your classifier predicted vs the true labels, broken down by class.[^4_4][^4_5][^4_6]

For a binary problem (e.g., “has sleep disorder” vs “no disorder”), it counts:

- True Positives (TP): model says “disorder” and it’s correct.
- False Positives (FP): model says “disorder” but actually none.
- False Negatives (FN): model says “no disorder” but there is one.
- True Negatives (TN): model says “no disorder” and it’s correct.[^4_5][^4_4]

This helps you see not just accuracy, but *where* the model gets confused (e.g., missing people with insomnia).

***

## What are MAE and RMSE?

Both are **error metrics for regression** (continuous targets like sleep quality).[^4_7][^4_8][^4_9]

- MAE (Mean Absolute Error):
    - Average of $|\text{prediction} - \text{true}|$.
    - “On average, how many points off am I?”
- RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error):
    - Square errors, average them, then take the square root.
    - Penalizes large errors more than MAE.[^4_8][^4_7]

In your project: “My model’s MAE is 0.8” ≈ on average, you’re 0.8 points away on the 1–10 sleep quality scale.

***

## Are linear and logistic two types of regression?

Yes, they’re both **regression models**, but used for different tasks.[^4_10][^4_11]

- **Linear regression**
    - Output: continuous number (e.g., predict sleep quality 1–10).
    - Fits a straight line (or hyperplane) to minimize squared error.[^4_11][^4_10]
- **Logistic regression**
    - Output: probability of a class (e.g., having a sleep disorder).
    - Used for **classification**, with a sigmoid to squash outputs to $[0,1]$.[^4_10][^4_11]

So they share the “regression” name and some math, but in practice you’ll treat one as your basic regressor and the other as your basic classifier.

# What exactly is modelling??

In this context, **modeling** is the step where you build a mathematical “rule” from data so it can make predictions on new, unseen cases.[^5_1][^5_2][^5_3][^5_4]

More concretely for your sleep project:

- You start with **examples**: each row has features (age, stress, activity, sleep duration, etc.) and a target (e.g., has a sleep disorder, or sleep quality score).[^5_5]
- You choose an **algorithm** (e.g., logistic regression, linear regression).
- You let the algorithm **learn parameters** from the data so that its outputs match the targets as well as possible (minimize error on the training set).[^5_2][^5_3]
- The result of this learning is a **model**: a function that takes inputs (a person’s lifestyle features) and outputs a prediction (probability of a disorder, or a quality score).[^5_6][^5_4][^5_1][^5_2]

So:

- “Doing EDA” = understanding the data.

# What is IQR??

IQR is the **Interquartile Range** – it measures how spread out the **middle 50%** of your data is.[^8_1][^8_2][^8_3]

- You take the 25th percentile (Q1) and the 75th percentile (Q3).
- $\text{IQR} = Q3 - Q1$.
- Example with your sleep duration:
    - Q1 ≈ 6.4 hours, Q3 ≈ 7.8 hours → IQR ≈ 1.4 hours, meaning the central half of people sleep within a 1.4‑hour band.[^8_4][^8_5][^8_6]
